Margaret, 58 years
TYPE 2 DIABETES · DYSLIPIDAEMIA
Clinical Snapshot
Referred by GP for dietary counselling. HbA1c 8.2%, total cholesterol 6.4 mmol/L, LDL 4.1 mmol/L. BMI 31.2 kg/m². Menopausal. Reports fatigue, frequent urination, and poor sleep. Recently retired, more sedentary than prior years.
Diet History Highlights
  • Skips breakfast most days
  • Large lunch (pasta/sandwich + juice)
  • Afternoon snack: crackers, cheese, biscuits
  • Big dinner, often with wine (3–4 nights/wk)
  • Low vegetable intake; fruit 1–2 pcs/day
  • Rarely cooks with legumes or wholegrains
Key Nutritional Problems
High refined carbohydrate load; low fibre; irregular meal timing; excess saturated fat; alcohol contributing to triglycerides.
Step 1 — Broad Explanation
Explain T2DM: insulin resistance, glucose regulation, long-term consequences of hyperglycaemia. Include: dyslipidaemia and how it relates to cardiovascular risk.
💬 "Our bodies produce insulin to help cells use glucose for energy. In type 2 diabetes, our cells stop responding well to that signal…"
Step 2 — Link to Margaret
Reference her HbA1c (8.2%), fatigue, LDL result (4.1), and weight gain since menopause. Note hormonal changes worsening insulin sensitivity.
💬 "In your case Margaret, your HbA1c tells us your average blood sugar has been elevated for several months now. The fatigue you mentioned makes total sense…"
Step 3 — Diet Link
Connect skipped breakfast → large lunch glucose spike; high refined carbs + low fibre; alcohol → triglycerides; saturated fat in afternoon snacks → LDL.
💬 "When I look at your food diary, the skipped breakfast followed by the large carbohydrate lunch is likely causing significant glucose spikes…"
Step 4 — Goals
3 goals: (a) regular, carbohydrate-spread meals; (b) increase fibre via vegies/legumes/wholegrains; (c) reduce saturated fat in snacks and alcohol on weeknights.
💬 "Today I'd like us to work on three things that will make a real difference to both your blood sugar and your cholesterol…"
🏃‍♂️
Jake, 22 years
IRON DEFICIENCY ANAEMIA
Clinical Snapshot
University student, endurance runner (60 km/wk). Referred by sports physician. Serum ferritin 6 µg/L (↓↓), Hb 108 g/L. Reports declining performance, breathlessness on runs, difficulty concentrating in lectures. Vegetarian for 2 years.
Diet History Highlights
  • No meat/fish; occasional egg (2–3/wk)
  • Breakfast: oats + soy milk + banana
  • Lunch: vegemite sandwich or leftover pasta
  • Dinner: vegetable stir fry with rice or tofu
  • High tea intake (4–5 cups/day, with meals)
  • Rarely eats legumes, seeds, or fortified foods
Key Nutritional Problems
Very low haem iron intake; poor dietary iron variety; tannins in tea inhibiting absorption; high physical demand increasing iron losses; no vitamin C pairing strategy.
Step 1 — Broad Explanation
Explain iron's role: oxygen transport via haemoglobin, energy production, immune function. Distinguish haem vs non-haem iron. Explain how iron stores deplete — stages of deficiency.
💬 "Iron is essential for making haemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen to your muscles and organs. Without enough iron…"
Step 2 — Link to Jake
Reference ferritin (6 µg/L), haemoglobin (108 g/L), performance decline, breathlessness, brain fog. Discuss athlete iron losses (foot-strike haemolysis, sweat losses) compounding dietary deficit.
💬 "Jake, your ferritin — your iron storage — is very low at 6. For an athlete at your training volume, we'd like to see at least 30–40. This is why your runs feel harder…"
Step 3 — Diet Link
Low non-haem iron variety; tea 4–5 cups daily with meals blocks absorption (tannins); no vitamin C strategy to enhance non-haem absorption; low total energy for training load.
💬 "Looking at your food diary, I can see a couple of things working against you. The tea you're having with every meal contains compounds called tannins that actually bind to plant iron and stop it being absorbed…"
Step 4 — Goals
Goals: (a) increase iron-rich plant foods (legumes, seeds, tofu, fortified cereals); (b) time tea away from meals; (c) pair iron foods with vitamin C sources; (d) discuss supplement need with GP.
💬 "There are three practical things we can do that will meaningfully improve your iron absorption. The good news is they're quite simple changes…"
👩‍💼
Sandra, 45 years
HYPERTENSION · OVERWEIGHT
Clinical Snapshot
Full-time nurse, single mother of 2. BP 158/96 mmHg on two readings. BMI 29.4 kg/m². No medication yet — GP wants to trial lifestyle intervention first. Reports high stress, poor sleep (5–6 hrs), emotional eating, and no time to exercise.
Diet History Highlights
  • High processed/convenience food intake
  • Deli meats, canned soups, sauces daily
  • Low fruit and vegetable intake (≤2 serves/day)
  • Minimal dairy or calcium-rich foods
  • 3–4 coffees/day with 2 sugars
  • Night snacking during stress (chips, chocolate)
Key Nutritional Problems
Very high sodium intake from processed foods; low potassium (low fruit/veg); low calcium and magnesium; high energy-dense, nutrient-poor snacks; excess caffeine potentially elevating BP.
Step 1 — Broad Explanation
Explain hypertension: how blood pressure works (systolic/diastolic), what drives elevated BP (vessel resistance, fluid volume, cardiac output). Role of sodium and potassium balance.
💬 "Blood pressure is essentially the force of blood pushing against your artery walls. When it stays consistently high, it puts strain on the heart and vessels…"
Step 2 — Link to Sandra
Reference her BP readings (158/96 — Stage 2), BMI, stress and sleep deprivation (cortisol → BP), risk of needing medication if not addressed.
💬 "In your case Sandra, your blood pressure at 158/96 is in the range where we really want to act now — before it progresses to needing daily medication…"
Step 3 — Diet Link
High sodium from deli meats/soups/sauces; low potassium from minimal fruit/veg (potassium counteracts sodium's BP effect); low calcium/magnesium; stress eating → excess energy.
💬 "When I look at your diet, the biggest driver I can see is sodium — it's coming mostly from processed foods like the deli meats and canned soups, often without us realising…"
Step 4 — Goals
Goals: (a) swap processed foods for lower-sodium alternatives 3x/week; (b) increase fruit and vegetable serves to 5+/day for potassium; (c) establish a low-sodium, easy dinner routine for busy nights.
💬 "I know you're incredibly time-poor, so I want to give you practical, realistic changes. Three things that research shows can lower BP within weeks…"

🔽 The DDR Funnel — Visual Overview

The funnel approach moves from broad to personalised. Start wide — explain the condition — then narrow down to the specific patient, their diet, and finally the goals. This scaffolded approach helps patients understand why change matters before hearing what to change.

1
Broad Condition / Problem Explanation
General pathophysiology — why it matters, what it does to the body
2
Narrow — Link to the Patient
Their symptoms, results, lifestyle. "In your case…"
3
Link to Diet / Nutrient Problem
Connect food diary findings to the condition's progression
4
Key Goals (Scaffolded)
1–3 clear, positive goals. Name them before explaining each.
🧠 Key Principles to Remember
🙋
Ask First

Always check what the patient already understands before you explain. You may be surprised — or you'll know exactly where to start.

🌟
Lead with Strengths

Open with 1–2 things the patient is already doing well. It builds trust and shows you've really listened to their story.

🔗
Always Personalise

Use their name. Use their results. Reference their specific diet diary entries. Generic information doesn't motivate change — personal relevance does.

🎯
Name Goals Before Strategies

Don't dive straight into strategies. Scaffold the intervention by naming all 3 goals first. This prevents info overwhelm and helps patients track the conversation.

⏱
Inpatient Rule

If you have <2 minutes, distil to: 1 brief DDR point (the most critical why) + 1–2 key strategies. Every word must earn its place.

💬
Confidence Check

Before summarising, ask: "How confident do you feel about making these changes?" Listen carefully — this reveals what extra support may be needed.

📋 Quick Reference: Condition → Key Nutrients
Condition Key Nutrients / Focus DDR Core Message
T2DM Total carbohydrate, glycaemic load, dietary fibre, saturated fat Carb type/timing/quantity drives blood glucose; fibre slows absorption
CVD / Dyslipidaemia Saturated fat, trans fat, omega-3, dietary fibre, plant sterols Dietary fat quality changes LDL/HDL ratio; fibre lowers LDL
Hypertension Sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, alcohol Sodium retains fluid → raises BP; potassium/Mg counteract this effect
Iron Deficiency Anaemia Haem/non-haem iron, vitamin C, inhibitors (tannins, phytates, Ca) Low iron stores → reduced oxygen delivery → fatigue, poor performance
CKD Protein, phosphorus, potassium, sodium, fluid (stage-dependent) Kidney can't excrete excess minerals → build-up causes complications
Coeliac Disease Gluten avoidance, iron, calcium, B vitamins, dietary fibre Gluten triggers intestinal damage → malabsorption of key nutrients
Osteoporosis Calcium, vitamin D, protein, magnesium, phosphorus Bone continuously remodels — dietary Ca/D insufficient = net bone loss
IBS FODMAPs, dietary fibre type (soluble vs insoluble), fluid Fermentable carbohydrates → excess gas/fluid in bowel → symptoms
