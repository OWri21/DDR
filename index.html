Opening Summary — Re-entering the Consultation
The bridge from assessment to intervention — summarise findings, acknowledge strengths, introduce the key problem
▼
☕
You've just stepped back in after the break. This is your re-entry moment. Before diving into the DDR, take 2–3 sentences to re-orient the patient, acknowledge what they're doing well, and signal that you're about to explain why today's goals matter. This is what transforms an awkward restart into a confident, patient-centred transition.
✦ A — Acknowledge What's Going Well (1–2 sentences)
🌟 Lead with a strength:
Open with something genuine and specific the patient is already doing well. This isn't flattery — it shows you've listened, builds rapport, and makes the patient more receptive to hearing about the problem.

"You've made some great progress since we last saw you — you've been [specific positive behaviour], which is really helping with [outcome]."

✦ Reference a specific behaviour from the diet history or their own goals
✦ Even if diet is largely problematic, find one genuine positive
✦ Keep it brief — 1–2 sentences maximum
0 characters
✦ B — Summarise Findings & Introduce the Key Problem (2–4 sentences)
🔍 The bridge statement:
Briefly summarise the key finding from the assessment and signal that you're about to explain why it matters and what can be done. This sets up the DDR and gives the patient a clear reason to keep listening.

"But if we look at the bigger picture, the main thing that stands out to me from everything you've shared today is [key problem]. I'd like to take a few minutes to explain what's happening and why it's worth addressing — and then we'll work together on some practical ways forward."

✦ One clear, specific problem — don't list everything
✦ Use language that signals partnership: "we'll work on…" not "you need to…"
✦ This sentence is the launchpad for Step 1 of the DDR — end it with a natural segue

Broad Problem / Condition Explanation

✦ Include key physiological context (e.g., how insulin resistance develops)
✦ Mention relevant symptoms or common presentations
✦ Keep it jargon-free — imagine explaining to a curious friend

3
The Mechanism — Diet → Physiology → Disease
Two distinct outputs: written clinical statement first, then verbal patient explanation
▼
⚠️ Step 3 = clinical reasoning — not dietary advice, not patient education The mechanism statement and the verbal explanation are two different outputs. Students commonly mix them up. Write the clinical statement first, then translate it for the patient. What gets marked in assessments is the written mechanism — concise, biological, third-person.
✦ A — Written Mechanism Statement (clinical language — what you write / what is assessed)
Format: [Dietary factor] → [Physiological process] → [Disease outcome]

✦ Start with the dietary factor — not the disease
✦ End with the disease outcome (e.g. "leading to iron deficiency anaemia")
✦ Written in third-person, clinical language
✦ Concise — if the mechanism is visible and accurate, you have enough. Less is more.

❌ Do NOT include in the mechanism statement: dietary recommendations ("increase haem iron"), client-facing or counselling language, descriptions of symptoms or fatigue, background nutrition education (food sources, serving sizes), or extra clinical context not part of the biological mechanism.

Model example — Iron Deficiency Anaemia:
"Low intake of haem iron reduces the availability of highly bioavailable iron for absorption. Concurrent consumption of calcium-rich foods inhibits intestinal iron absorption through competitive interactions, further reducing iron bioavailability. Together, reduced iron intake and impaired absorption limit iron availability for haemoglobin synthesis, leading to iron deficiency anaemia."
0 characters
✦ B — Verbal Explanation to the Patient (accessible language — for the consultation)
Now translate the mechanism into language the patient can understand:
Use the clinical statement above as your foundation. Reframe it accessibly — use their name, reference specific examples from their food diary, and connect it to their experience.

"The reason this matters is because [accessible version of mechanism]… and when I look at your food diary specifically, I can see [specific examples] that show this pattern…"

✦ This is where personalisation, empathy, and diet history examples live
✦ This is NOT what gets written as the mechanism in your chart note or assessment — it is how you communicate it clinically

Key Goal(s) & Positive Framing
Explain 1–3 clear, motivating goals and how each one will help
▼
🎯 Set the stage for change:
Scaffold the intervention by naming the goals BEFORE diving into strategies. Briefly describe how each goal helps — this prevents everything blending into one overwhelming recommendation.

"So what we can work on today is: a) ___, b) ___, and c) ___. Let me walk you through each one and why it matters for your [condition/goal]…"

✦ Keep to 1–3 broad goals
✦ Ask the patient if they have anything they'd like to add
✦ Use hopeful, empowering language — change IS possible

Margaret, 58 years
TYPE 2 DIABETES · DYSLIPIDAEMIA
Clinical Snapshot
Referred by GP for dietary counselling. HbA1c 8.2%, total cholesterol 6.4 mmol/L, LDL 4.1 mmol/L. BMI 31.2 kg/m². Menopausal. Reports fatigue, frequent urination, and poor sleep. Recently retired, more sedentary than prior years.
Diet History Highlights
  • Skips breakfast most days
  • Large lunch (pasta/sandwich + juice)
  • Afternoon snack: crackers, cheese, biscuits
  • Big dinner, often with wine (3–4 nights/wk)
  • Low vegetable intake; fruit 1–2 pcs/day
  • Rarely cooks with legumes or wholegrains
Key Nutritional Problems
High refined carbohydrate load; low fibre; irregular meal timing; excess saturated fat; alcohol contributing to triglycerides.
Opening Summary — Re-entering the Consultation
Acknowledge 1–2 genuine positives (e.g. home cooking, showing up), then bridge to the key finding (HbA1c, weight gain) and signal what's coming.
💬 "Margaret, before we get into things — I noticed you've been cooking dinner at home most nights, which is a real strength. But looking at your results today, the main thing I'd like to walk you through is what's been happening with your blood sugar and why it matters for how you've been feeling…"
Step 1 — Broad Explanation
Explain T2DM: insulin resistance, glucose regulation, long-term consequences of hyperglycaemia. Include: dyslipidaemia and how it relates to cardiovascular risk.
💬 "Our bodies produce insulin to help cells use glucose for energy. In type 2 diabetes, our cells stop responding well to that signal…"
Step 2 — Link to Margaret
Reference her HbA1c (8.2%), fatigue, LDL result (4.1), and weight gain since menopause. Note hormonal changes worsening insulin sensitivity.
💬 "In your case Margaret, your HbA1c tells us your average blood sugar has been elevated for several months now. The fatigue you mentioned makes total sense…"
Step 3A — Mechanism Statement (clinical language)
Format: [Dietary factor] → [Physiological process] → [Disease outcome]. Third-person, no advice, no patient language.
📋 "Habitual consumption of large refined carbohydrate meals with irregular timing produces repeated postprandial glucose spikes, driving hyperinsulinaemia and progressive insulin resistance, resulting in poor glycaemic control. High saturated fat intake from processed meats and full-fat dairy raises LDL cholesterol through upregulation of hepatic cholesterol synthesis. Excess alcohol consumption elevates serum triglycerides via increased hepatic VLDL production, contributing to combined dyslipidaemia."
STEP 3B — Verbal Explanation (patient-facing)
💬 "When I look at your food diary, the skipped breakfast followed by the large carbohydrate lunch is likely causing significant glucose spikes — and over time, this pattern is what drives insulin resistance. The afternoon snacks and the wine most nights are also contributing to your cholesterol and triglyceride results…"
Step 4 — Goals
3 goals: (a) regular, carbohydrate-spread meals; (b) increase fibre via vegies/legumes/wholegrains; (c) reduce saturated fat in snacks and alcohol on weeknights.
💬 "Today I'd like us to work on three things that will make a real difference to both your blood sugar and your cholesterol…"

Jake, 22 years
IRON DEFICIENCY ANAEMIA
Clinical Snapshot
University student, endurance runner (60 km/wk). Referred by sports physician. Serum ferritin 6 µg/L (↓↓), Hb 108 g/L. Reports declining performance, breathlessness on runs, difficulty concentrating in lectures. Vegetarian for 2 years.
Diet History Highlights
  • No meat/fish; occasional egg (2–3/wk)
  • Breakfast: oats + soy milk + banana
  • Lunch: vegemite sandwich or leftover pasta
  • Dinner: vegetable stir fry with rice or tofu
  • High tea intake (4–5 cups/day, with meals)
  • Rarely eats legumes, seeds, or fortified foods
Key Nutritional Problems
Very low haem iron intake; poor dietary iron variety; tannins in tea inhibiting absorption; high physical demand increasing iron losses; no vitamin C pairing strategy.
Opening Summary — Re-entering the Consultation
Acknowledge his structured eating and training commitment. Bridge to the iron results and performance decline as the key problem.
💬 "Jake, I want to start by saying your eating structure is actually really solid, and maintaining a vegetarian diet while training 60km a week takes real discipline. But when I look at your blood results, there's a clear picture around your iron levels that explains a lot of what's been happening with your runs…"
Step 1 — Broad Explanation
Explain iron's role: oxygen transport via haemoglobin, energy production, immune function. Distinguish haem vs non-haem iron. Explain how iron stores deplete — stages of deficiency.
💬 "Iron is essential for making haemoglobin — the protein in red blood cells that carries oxygen to your muscles and organs. Without enough iron…"
Step 2 — Link to Jake
Reference ferritin (6 µg/L), haemoglobin (108 g/L), performance decline, breathlessness, brain fog. Discuss athlete iron losses (foot-strike haemolysis, sweat losses) compounding dietary deficit.
💬 "Jake, your ferritin — your iron storage — is very low at 6. For an athlete at your training volume, we'd like to see at least 30–40. This is why your runs feel harder…"
Step 3A — Mechanism Statement (clinical language)
Format: [Dietary factor] → [Physiological process] → [Disease outcome]. Third-person, no advice, no patient language.
📋 "Low dietary intake of non-haem iron provides insufficient substrate for intestinal absorption. Concurrent consumption of tea at mealtimes introduces tannins and polyphenols that form insoluble complexes with non-haem iron in the gastrointestinal tract, directly inhibiting absorption. Absence of vitamin C pairing further limits non-haem iron bioavailability. Together, inadequate iron intake and impaired absorption reduce iron availability for haemoglobin synthesis, leading to iron deficiency anaemia."
STEP 3B — Verbal Explanation (patient-facing)
💬 "The tea you're having with every meal contains compounds called tannins that bind to plant iron and stop it being absorbed before your body can use it. And because you're not eating meat, the iron in your diet is already harder to absorb — so it's a compounding problem. There's no vitamin C in your meals to help offset that either…"
Step 4 — Goals
Goals: (a) increase iron-rich plant foods (legumes, seeds, tofu, fortified cereals); (b) time tea away from meals; (c) pair iron foods with vitamin C sources; (d) discuss supplement need with GP.
💬 "There are three practical things we can do that will meaningfully improve your iron absorption. The good news is they're quite simple changes…"
👩‍💼
Sandra, 45 years
HYPERTENSION · OVERWEIGHT
Clinical Snapshot
Full-time nurse, single mother of 2. BP 158/96 mmHg on two readings. BMI 29.4 kg/m². No medication yet — GP wants to trial lifestyle intervention first. Reports high stress, poor sleep (5–6 hrs), emotional eating, and no time to exercise.
Diet History Highlights
  • High processed/convenience food intake
  • Deli meats, canned soups, sauces daily
  • Low fruit and vegetable intake (≤2 serves/day)
  • Minimal dairy or calcium-rich foods
  • 3–4 coffees/day with 2 sugars
  • Night snacking during stress (chips, chocolate)
Key Nutritional Problems
Very high sodium intake from processed foods; low potassium (low fruit/veg); low calcium and magnesium; high energy-dense, nutrient-poor snacks; excess caffeine potentially elevating BP.
Opening Summary — Re-entering the Consultation
Acknowledge her awareness of needing to change and her commitment despite life demands. Bridge to BP as the key problem and signal a practical, realistic approach.
💬 "Sandra, I can see that you're genuinely committed to making changes, and the fact that you've come in today with everything you're juggling means a lot. What I'd like to do is explain what's driving your blood pressure results, because I think it'll make the changes we discuss feel a lot more purposeful — and they're changes we can fit around your real life…"
Step 1 — Broad Explanation
Explain hypertension: how blood pressure works (systolic/diastolic), what drives elevated BP (vessel resistance, fluid volume, cardiac output). Role of sodium and potassium balance.
💬 "Blood pressure is essentially the force of blood pushing against your artery walls. When it stays consistently high, it puts strain on the heart and vessels…"
Step 2 — Link to Sandra
Reference her BP readings (158/96 — Stage 2), BMI, stress and sleep deprivation (cortisol → BP), risk of needing medication if not addressed.
💬 "In your case Sandra, your blood pressure at 158/96 is in the range where we really want to act now — before it progresses to needing daily medication…"
Step 3A — Mechanism Statement (clinical language)
Format: [Dietary factor] → [Physiological process] → [Disease outcome]. Third-person, no advice, no patient language.
📋 "High dietary sodium intake from processed and convenience foods promotes renal sodium and water retention, increasing extracellular fluid volume and elevating blood pressure via increased cardiac preload and systemic vascular resistance. Concurrent low potassium intake from inadequate fruit and vegetable consumption diminishes the counter-regulatory effect of potassium on sodium-mediated vasoconstriction. Together, elevated sodium and insufficient potassium intake sustain chronically elevated blood pressure, consistent with Stage 2 hypertension."
STEP 3B — Verbal Explanation (patient-facing)
💬 "When I look at your diet Sandra, the biggest driver I can see is sodium — it's mostly coming from the deli meats, canned soups, and sauces, often without us realising. Sodium makes your body hold onto fluid, which pushes your blood pressure up. And the low fruit and vegetable intake means you're missing the potassium that actually helps counteract that effect…"
Step 4 — Goals
Goals: (a) swap processed foods for lower-sodium alternatives 3x/week; (b) increase fruit and vegetable serves to 5+/day for potassium; (c) establish a low-sodium, easy dinner routine for busy nights.
💬 "I know you're incredibly time-poor, so I want to give you practical, realistic changes. Three things that research shows can lower BP within weeks…"

🧠 Key Principles to Remember
🔬
Mechanism Is the Heart of the DDR

Step 3 must explain the biological pathway — [dietary pattern] → [physiological process] → [disease effect]. Without this, you have a list of dietary problems, not a diet-disease relationship. The mechanism is what makes your clinical reasoning defensible.

🙋
Ask First

Always check what the patient already understands before you explain. You may be surprised — or you'll know exactly where to start.

🌟
Lead with Strengths

Open with 1–2 things the patient is already doing well. It builds trust and shows you've really listened to their story.

🔗
Always Personalise

Use their name. Use their results. Reference their specific diet diary entries. Generic information doesn't motivate change — personal relevance does.

🎯
Name Goals Before Strategies

Don't dive straight into strategies. Scaffold the intervention by naming all 3 goals first. This prevents info overwhelm and helps patients track the conversation.

⏱
Inpatient Rule

If you have <2 minutes, distil to: 1 brief DDR point (the most critical why) + 1–2 key strategies. Every word must earn its place.

💬
Confidence Check

Before summarising, ask: "How confident do you feel about making these changes?" Listen carefully — this reveals what extra support may be needed.

Hypertension Sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, alcohol Sodium retains fluid → raises BP; potassium/Mg counteract this effect
Iron Deficiency Anaemia Haem/non-haem iron, vitamin C, inhibitors (tannins, phytates, Ca) Low iron stores → reduced oxygen delivery → fatigue, poor performance
